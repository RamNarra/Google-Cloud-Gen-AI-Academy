05 What Are AI Agents? — Prashanth's Full Definition

At roughly the 18-minute mark, Koshik hands over to Prashanth, and the session shifts completely in tone and depth. What follows is one of the cleaner explanations of agents given in a public format. It is worth understanding every part of it because this conceptual foundation is what the entire three-track curriculum builds on.

Prashanth begins by acknowledging the hype directly and without embarrassment:

"Agents have been a buzzword since maybe the last year, maybe even before, but definitely it was a buzzword in 2025, and we are continuing into 2026. This is here — this is not something that went up the hype cycle, went down, and people forgot about it. We haven't exploited the full potential of agents."

He then gives the formal definition. The way Google Cloud thinks about agents:

Prashanth's Definition of an Agent

An agent is an application — just like any software you have built before. But it has three specific characteristics: it uses a generative AI model as its brain to make decisions; it can act independently based on its own reasoning; and it pursues a user-specified goal, not a fixed sequence of instructions.

The key phrase is goal-specified rather than instruction-specified. With traditional software, you write: "if the user clicks this button, do this sequence of steps." With an agent, you write: "achieve this outcome for the user" — and the agent figures out which steps to take.

The Three Properties That Make Agents Different

Prashanth identifies three specific properties that distinguish agents from all previous software paradigms:

Property 1 — Flexibility

Traditional software follows hard-coded algorithmic paths. Every edge case must be anticipated and handled explicitly by the developer. Agents do not need this. Given a goal, an agent can handle edge cases it has never encountered before, adapt to unexpected inputs, and navigate situations that were never programmed into it. It reasons about what to do rather than executing what it was told.

Prashanth gives a vivid contrast: a traditional customer support system asks you to press 1 for billing issues, press 2 for delivery problems, and so on — every possible scenario pre-mapped by a developer. An agentic customer support system simply listens to what you say in natural language, understands your problem, and navigates toward the right resolution on its own.

Property 2 — Outcome Focus

This is the most important conceptual shift. You do not tell an agent how to do something. You tell it what you want. The agent determines the path. Prashanth frames it this way: agents are led by the outcome, not the approach. This also makes prototyping faster — because you are not scripting every step, you can get a working agent up and running quickly and observe how it reaches the outcome.

Property 3 — Proactivity

Agents can be triggered by schedules, events, or external signals — not just by a human typing something. You can build agents that monitor systems overnight, watch for a specific market signal, check whether a vulnerability has been patched, or summarise your emails every morning without you asking. This is what Prashanth means by proactive: agents can deliver intelligence into your life without waiting to be prompted.

06 Where Agents Make Sense — and Where They Do Not

This section is one of the most intellectually honest parts of the entire session. Prashanth explicitly tells you when not to use agents. This is unusual — most product and developer advocates will push their technology into every scenario. Prashanth does not, and this builds significant credibility for what he says next.

Where Agents Are a Strong Fit

Use Case TypeWhy Agents Work Well Here
Personalised learning systemsThe agent adapts to the learner's style, pace, and topic preferences dynamically. No two users follow the same path.
Real-time business intelligenceAgent uses live data signals to adjust behaviour. It is not answering from a static database — it is reacting to what is happening right now.
Customer experience systemsReplace brittle decision trees with agents that understand natural language, identify what the user needs, and route appropriately.
Cybersecurity monitoring(Prashanth confirms this explicitly in Q&A) — agents can triage incidents, monitor for new vulnerabilities, and check existing systems automatically.
Any goal-oriented, multi-step workflowIf the task requires making multiple decisions, calling multiple systems, and adapting to what comes back — agents are the right tool.

Where Agents Are Overkill or Wrong

This is the crucial part. Prashanth identifies three clear situations where you should not build an agent:

Situation 1 — Simple Prompt-Response Tasks

If you have a large block of text and you want to summarise it, or you want to generate a document from a detailed system instruction, you do not need an agent. You need an API call. The task is: send prompt → receive output. There is no decision-making, no tool-calling, no iteration. An agent would add complexity with zero benefit.

Situation 2 — Pure Q&A from a Knowledge Base

If your system does one thing — a user asks a question, you retrieve relevant material from a knowledge base, and you return an answer — you need a RAG architecture, not an agent. RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) is the correct tool here. It is simpler, cheaper, faster, and more predictable.

"Building chatbots that can tap into a RAG system is good enough. You don't necessarily need agents that are goal-oriented or need to be autonomous in these cases."

Situation 3 — Regulated Environments Requiring Full Explainability

In some industries — banking, healthcare, insurance, certain areas of government — a system must be able to explain, precisely, why it took every decision it took. Traditional machine learning classifiers and rule-based systems can do this. Agentic systems, by their nature, cannot. The reasoning of a large language model is not fully traceable. In these environments, Prashanth says bluntly: use predictive AI, not generative AI. Stick to classifiers and explicit decision logic.

The Mental Test Before Building

Before you build an agent, ask yourself: does this task require the system to make independent decisions, adapt to unpredictable inputs, and pursue a goal across multiple steps? If yes — agent. If no — an API call, a RAG system, or a traditional ML model is more appropriate. Unnecessary agents are slower, harder to debug, and more expensive.

07 The Challenges of Agentic Systems

Prashanth spends a substantial portion of the session on this topic. It is rare to hear a Google speaker acknowledge the weaknesses of a Google-promoted technology with this level of frankness. Every point he raises reflects a real engineering challenge you will encounter when you build agents.

Challenge 1 — Non-Determinism

Large language models are inherently non-deterministic. You can send the same input twice and get two different outputs. This is not a bug — it is a fundamental characteristic of how these models work. But it means you cannot guarantee that your agent will behave the same way every time it runs. Prashanth is explicit:

"If you need systems to behave exactly the same every single time, there is an expectation mismatch. I am not saying you should or should not expect it, but there is an expectation mismatch if you expect agents to behave in a predictable way."

For cybersecurity and regulated workloads, this is a significant concern. An agent that sometimes identifies a threat and sometimes does not — with the same input — is not production-safe without additional layers around it.

Challenge 2 — Immature Error Handling and Resilience

Agentic systems are newer than traditional software systems. The tooling, frameworks, and patterns for handling failures gracefully are still being developed. In a rule-based system, you can write explicit try/catch blocks, define fallback paths, and guarantee that the system recovers from every anticipated failure. In an agentic system, the flow is dynamic — the agent decides what to do next, and it may choose a path that leads to an unexpected failure state with no way out.

Prashanth identifies this as partly a mindset problem: developers coming from traditional software backgrounds try to build agents the way they would build any other software — with rigid error handling and predictable flow control. That approach does not map cleanly onto agents. You have to design for resilience differently: give the agent instructions on how to recover, build in self-correction loops, and accept that some failures will happen unpredictably.

Challenge 3 — Security and Privacy Are Still Evolving

Security practices for agentic systems are not yet as mature as they are for traditional software. Questions like: how do you prevent prompt injection attacks? how do you prevent an agent from being manipulated by a malicious tool response? how do you ensure sensitive data is not leaked through a tool call? — these are active research and engineering problems. They are not solved. Prashanth says directly: "Practices like security and privacy are still evolving areas in the world of agentic systems."

Challenge 4 — Traceability and Debuggability

When a traditional software bug occurs, you can trace it: line 47 of module X called function Y with argument Z and the output was wrong. The causality is clear. When an agent behaves unexpectedly, you often cannot do this. The large language model's reasoning — the chain of thought that led it to call a particular tool or produce a particular output — may not be fully inspectable. You can see what it did, but tracing why it did it is significantly harder.

Prashanth's Practical Guidance

Do not treat agentic systems the way you would treat traditional software. Build in checks and balances. Design your agent so that it can self-correct — let it recognise when it has gone off-path and find its way back. Use ADK's built-in evaluation tools to test agent behaviour systematically. Accept some level of non-determinism and design around it rather than fighting it.

08 The Architecture of Any Agentic Application

Having covered what agents are, where they fit, and what their challenges are, Prashanth moves to the architecture question: if you have decided to build an agent, what does the infrastructure look like?

He identifies three things that every production agentic application requires:

Every agentic application needs exactly three things:
① Runtime + ② Model (Brain) + ③ Tools (Hands & Legs)
Cloud Run  ·  Agent Engine             Gemini 3, Gemma, others         APIs, databases, MCP servers

① The Runtime — Where the Agent Lives

Your agent needs to run somewhere that is always available — not your laptop, which shuts down, goes offline, and is only accessible to you. Prashanth uses a pointed analogy:

"It's like you built an HTML page on your personal machine and said, 'Okay, everyone come and use it.' That's not how it scales."

The runtime for this programme is Cloud Run — Google's serverless, container-based platform. It is always on, scales automatically, and requires no infrastructure management. For those who want more infrastructure control, GKE (Google Kubernetes Engine) is also available. For a runtime specifically optimised for agents, Google has built Agent Engine — a managed service for running agents in production.

② The Model — The Brain

The model is what provides the intelligence. Without a model, there is no reasoning, no decision-making, no language understanding. Gemini 3 is Prashanth's recommended starting point — it is designed specifically for agentic workflows and for using tools. He makes an important note about model selection: start with a cost-effective model like Gemini Flash during development. Build your evaluation framework first, then swap to a higher-capability model if needed and compare against your evals. Never pick a model without first having a way to measure whether it is performing correctly for your use case.

③ Tools — The Hands and Legs

Tools are what allow the agent to interact with the world beyond the model's training data. Without tools, an agent can only reason and respond from what it already knows — which is static and may be outdated. Tools give the agent the ability to read from databases, call APIs, search the web, interact with other services, and even delegate to other agents. The protocol that standardises how agents talk to tools is MCP — Model Context Protocol — which is covered in Track 2.

09 What You Will Learn Across the Three Tracks

Prashanth walks through the specific technical skills each track delivers. Together, they map to the full architecture of a production agentic system.

Track 1 — ADK and Cloud Run

You will learn to build agents using ADK (the Agent Development Kit) in Python. ADK supports multiple agent types — sequential, parallel, loop — and handles orchestration, tool integration, memory, and deployment. You will also learn to use Cloud Run — how to deploy your agent as a serverless service, how to observe what is happening in production, and how to scale. You will go from running an agent locally to having it live on a public URL.

Track 2 — MCP (Model Context Protocol)

MCP is the industry-standard protocol for connecting agents to external tools and data sources. Track 2 teaches you to build MCP servers, connect your agents to real-world APIs and databases via MCP, and use MCP Toolbox — Google's library for common integrations. Cloud Run is also a great place to host your MCP servers, so this track builds directly on Track 1.

Track 3 — AI-Ready Databases and Agent Memory

Agents are stateless by default — they do not remember what happened in the previous session unless you give them a database. Track 3 teaches you to use AlloyDB and Cloud SQL — PostgreSQL-compatible databases that are vector-ready and AI-ready. You will learn how to give your agent persistent memory, store user context across sessions, and handle the data infrastructure that makes a production agentic application genuinely useful over time.

Prashanth on Observability — Across All Tracks

Regardless of which track you choose, you will learn about observability and monitoring — how to trace what tools are being called, what information is being passed between parts of the agent, and whether the agent is behaving as expected. This is Google Cloud's operations suite applied to agentic systems. It is what separates a hobby project from a production deployment.

11 The Guided Challenge-Based Learning Philosophy

Before the Q&A, Prashanth makes an interesting meta-point. He says the programme itself is designed on an agentic principle — it is outcome-focused, not instruction-focused. This is not just a clever analogy. It has direct implications for how you should approach the curriculum.

"This programme is also outcome-focused. You are not just sitting through lectures. You will go through guided challenge-based learning — you will do hands-on, complete exercises, solve assessments and challenges, implement and submit projects, and challenge yourself with hackathons."

The intended outcome is not just that you understand agents theoretically. The intended outcome is that you leave the programme with market-ready projects and skills that you can immediately apply or showcase. The learning is structured to produce tangible, demonstrable work — the kind of portfolio output you can show in a job interview, on a LinkedIn post, or in a GitHub repository.

12 Live Q&A — Six Questions Answered in Full

The final third of the session is a live Q&A where Prashanth answers questions from participants. These answers contain practical details that do not appear in the main talk and are worth reading carefully.

What are the prerequisites for ADK? What will we actually learn?

The only genuine prerequisite is the ability to program. If you can read and write Python, you are well-positioned — the courses are Python-focused. ADK supports other languages, but the workshops use Python. You do not need prior machine learning knowledge, mathematics beyond basic programming, or any prior Gen AI experience.

What you will learn: how to build agents with ADK; how to deploy agents to Cloud Run; how to connect agents to tools using MCP; how to test and observe your agent using ADK's local development UI (which has a built-in evaluation tab for running test cases); and how to use ADK's debugging capabilities to understand what is happening when your agent runs.

Which database is recommended for agentic AI?

Prashanth's answer: the database you are already using is probably fine, because all Google Cloud databases are vector-ready and AI-ready. However, the curriculum will focus on PostgreSQL-compliant databases — specifically Cloud SQL and AlloyDB — because they are the most widely used and require no new learning. If you already know PostgreSQL, you are already prepared.

How much does Cloud Run cost? Will we get charged?

Cloud Run has a generous free tier. Prashanth estimates upwards of a thousand calls per day are free — you can verify the exact numbers in the documentation. For the learning and development phase of this programme, you should not be charged anything. The programme also provides credits. If you eventually build something popular that drives real traffic beyond the free tier, you will start to incur costs — but that would be a good problem to have.

Can AI orchestration be used in cybersecurity and high-end sales?

Prashanth's answer is an enthusiastic yes for both. For cybersecurity specifically, he gives concrete examples: agents that triage security incidents, monitor systems for new vulnerabilities and check whether they affect your infrastructure, observe and alert on suspicious patterns. He also notes that Google Cloud has out-of-the-box agentic security products — bot detection, MCP server protection, and others — for teams that want managed security rather than DIY.

For sales, he mentions agents that use real-time signals and market data to adapt sales and marketing plans dynamically. The common thread is: any domain where goals are clear, data is available, and decisions need to be made adaptively in real time is a strong candidate for agentic systems.

How do I pick the right model for my use case? How do I test which one is best?

Prashanth recommends a specific workflow: start with Gemini Flash — the cost-effective, fast model. Build your system. Before you worry about which model to use, build your evaluation framework first. Define what "good output" looks like for your specific use case. Create test cases — a set of inputs and expected outputs. Run those evals against your agent regularly as you iterate.

Once you have that evaluation framework, then you can swap models and compare. Run Gemini Flash against your evals. Run Gemini Pro against the same evals. Compare performance against cost. Without an eval framework, you are guessing. With one, you are engineering.

ADK has an evaluation tab built into its local development UI for exactly this purpose. Use it from the beginning.

Can agents be used in regulated environments like healthcare or finance?

The answer is nuanced. Prashanth says it is not a binary yes or no — it depends on what the specific regulation requires. Two common regulatory constraints he addresses:

Data residency: If your regulation requires that data stays within a country's borders, you can still use agents — but you must deploy all components (model, database, APIs) within the compliant region. Google Cloud has regions in Mumbai and Delhi for India, and multiple regions across APAC. Staying within a region is technically straightforward.

Human-in-the-loop requirements: In healthcare, you may be prohibited from giving medical recommendations directly to patients without physician review. In this case, you can still use an agent — but you must ensure the agent's output goes to a human reviewer before reaching the end user. Design your agent with a mandatory human review step. Do not make it fully autonomous in these contexts.

Explainability requirements: If the regulation requires you to explain every decision, use ADK's workflow agents (sequential, rule-based flows) rather than fully autonomous LLM agents. You trade some flexibility for traceability.

Closing Thought from Prashanth

The session closes with one idea that Prashanth returns to repeatedly throughout: agents are outcome-focused, and so is this programme. The goal is not just that you understand these concepts — it is that you produce something real, deployable, and demonstrable. The best participants will not just complete the steps; they will think carefully about what problem they want to solve, use the tracks as infrastructure, and build something they are genuinely proud to show.
